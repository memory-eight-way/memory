XIII. THE USE OF SPIES
XIII. TH_ US_ O_ SPIE_

1-1. Sun Tzu said: Raising a host of a hundred thousand men and marching them great distances entails heavy loss on the people and a drain on the resources of the State.

1-2. Th_ dail_ expenditur_ wil_ amoun_ t_ _ thousan_ ounce_ o_ silver_

1-4. As many as seven hundred thousand families will be impeded in their labor.
1-4. A_ man_ a_ seve_ hundre_ thousan_ familie_ wil_ b_ impede_ i_ thei_ labor_

2-1. Hostile armies may face each other for years, striving for the victory which is decided in a single day.
2-1. Hostil_ armie_ ma_ fac_ eac_ othe_ fo_ years_ strivin_ fo_ th_ victor_ whic_ i_ decide_ i_ _ singl_ day_

2-2. This being so, to remain in ignorance of the enemy's condition simply because one grudges the outlay of a hundred ounces of silver in honors and emoluments, is the height of inhumanity.

3. One who acts thus is no leader of men, no present help to his sovereign, no master of victory.
3. On_ wh_ act_ thu_ i_ n_ leade_ o_ men_ n_ presen_ hel_ t_ hi_ sovereign_ n_ maste_ o_ victory_

4. Thus, what enables the wise sovereign and the good general to strike and conquer, and achieve things beyond the reach of ordinary men, is foreknowledge.
4. Thus_ wha_ enable_ th_ wis_ sovereig_ an_ th_ goo_ genera_ t_ strik_ an_ conquer_ an_ achiev_ thing_ beyon_ th_ reac_ o_ ordinar_ men_ i_ foreknowledge_

6. Knowledge of the enemy's dispositions can only be obtained from other men.

7. Hence the use of spies, of whom there are five classes: (1) Local spies; (2) inward spies; (3) converted spies; (4) doomed spies; (5) surviving spies.
7. Henc_ th_ us_ o_ spies_ o_ who_ ther_ ar_ fiv_ classes_ (1_ Loca_ spies_ (2_ inwar_ spies_ (3_ converte_ spies_ (4_ doome_ spies_ (5_ survivin_ spies_

8-1. When these five kinds of spy are all at work, none can discover the secret system.
8-1. Whe_ thes_ fiv_ kind_ o_ sp_ ar_ al_ a_ work_ non_ ca_ discove_ th_ secre_ system_

8-2. This is called "divine manipulation of the threads."
8-2. Thi_ i_ calle_ "divin_ manipulatio_ o_ th_ threads._

8-3. It is the sovereign's most precious faculty.
8-3. I_ i_ th_ sovereign'_ mos_ preciou_ faculty_

9. Having local spies means employing the services of the inhabitants of a district.
9. Havin_ loca_ spie_ mean_ employin_ th_ service_ o_ th_ inhabitant_ o_ _ district_

10. Having inward spies, making use of officials of the enemy.
10. Havin_ inwar_ spies_ makin_ us_ o_ official_ o_ th_ enemy_

11. Having converted spies, getting hold of the enemy's spies and using them for our own purposes.
11. Havin_ converte_ spies_ gettin_ hol_ o_ th_ enemy'_ spie_ an_ usin_ the_ fo_ ou_ ow_ purposes_

12. Having doomed spies, doing certain things openly for purposes of deception, and allowing our spies to know of them and report them to the enemy.
12. Havin_ doome_ spies_ doin_ certai_ thing_ openl_ fo_ purpose_ o_ deception_ an_ allowin_ ou_ spie_ t_ kno_ o_ the_ an_ repor_ the_ t_ th_ enemy_

13. Surviving spies, finally, are those who bring back news from the enemy's camp.
13. Survivin_ spies_ finally_ ar_ thos_ wh_ brin_ bac_ new_ fro_ th_ enemy'_ camp_

14-1. Hence it is that which none in the whole army are more intimate relations to be maintained than with spies.
14-1. Henc_ i_ i_ tha_ whic_ non_ i_ th_ whol_ arm_ ar_ mor_ intimat_ relation_ t_ b_ maintaine_ tha_ wit_ spies_

14-2. None should be more liberally rewarded.
14-2. Non_ shoul_ b_ mor_ liberall_ rewarded_

14-3. In no other business should greater secrecy be preserved.
14-3. I_ n_ othe_ busines_ shoul_ greate_ secrec_ b_ preserved_

15. Spies cannot be usefully employed without a certain intuitive sagacity.
15. Spie_ canno_ b_ usefull_ employe_ withou_ _ certai_ intuitiv_ sagacity_

16. They cannot be properly managed without benevolence and straightforwardness.
16. The_ canno_ b_ properl_ manage_ withou_ benevolenc_ an_ straightforwardness_

17. Without subtle ingenuity of mind, one cannot make certain of the truth of their reports.
17. Withou_ subtl_ ingenuit_ o_ mind_ on_ canno_ mak_ certai_ o_ th_ trut_ o_ thei_ reports_

18. Be subtle! be subtle! and use your spies for every kind of business.
18. B_ subtle_ b_ subtle_ an_ us_ you_ spie_ fo_ ever_ kin_ o_ business_

19. If a secret piece of news is divulged by a spy before the time is ripe, he must be put to death together with the man to whom the secret was told.
19. I_ _ secre_ piec_ o_ new_ i_ divulge_ b_ _ sp_ befor_ th_ tim_ i_ ripe_ h_ mus_ b_ pu_ t_ deat_ togethe_ wit_ th_ ma_ t_ who_ th_ secre_ wa_ told_

20-1. Whether the object be to crush an army, to storm a city, or to assassinate an individual, it is always necessary to begin by finding out the names of the attendants, the aides-de-camp, and door-keepers and sentries of the general in command.
20-1. Whethe_ th_ objec_ b_ t_ crus_ a_ army_ t_ stor_ _ city_ o_ t_ assassinat_ a_ individual_ i_ i_ alway_ necessar_ t_ begi_ b_ findin_ ou_ th_ name_ o_ th_ attendants_ th_ aides-de-camp_ an_ door-keeper_ an_ sentrie_ o_ th_ genera_ i_ command_

20-2. Our spies must be commissioned to ascertain these.
20-2. Ou_ spie_ mus_ b_ commissione_ t_ ascertai_ these_

21-1. The enemy's spies who have come to spy on us must be sought out, tempted with bribes, led away and comfortably housed.
21-1. Th_ enemy'_ spie_ wh_ hav_ com_ t_ sp_ o_ u_ mus_ b_ sough_ out_ tempte_ wit_ bribes_ le_ awa_ an_ comfortabl_ housed_

21-2. Thus they will become converted spies and available for our service.
21-2. Thu_ the_ wil_ becom_ converte_ spie_ an_ availabl_ fo_ ou_ service_

22. It is through the information brought by the converted spy that we are able to acquire and employ local and inward spies.
22. I_ i_ throug_ th_ informatio_ brough_ b_ th_ converte_ sp_ tha_ w_ ar_ abl_ t_ acquir_ an_ emplo_ loca_ an_ inwar_ spies_

23. It is owing to his information, again, that we can cause the doomed spy to carry false tidings to the enemy.
23. I_ i_ owin_ t_ hi_ information_ again_ tha_ w_ ca_ caus_ th_ doome_ sp_ t_ carr_ fals_ tiding_ t_ th_ enemy_

24. Lastly, it is by his information that the surviving spy can be used on appointed occasions.

25-1. The end and aim of spying in all its five varieties is knowledge of the enemy; and this knowledge can only be derived, in the first instance, from the converted spy.
25-1. Th_ en_ an_ ai_ o_ spyin_ i_ al_ it_ fiv_ varietie_ i_ knowledg_ o_ th_ enemy_ an_ thi_ knowledg_ ca_ onl_ b_ derived_ i_ th_ firs_ instance_ fro_ th_ converte_ spy_

25-2. Hence it is essential that the converted spy be treated with the utmost liberality.
25-2. Henc_ i_ i_ essentia_ tha_ th_ converte_ sp_ b_ treate_ wit_ th_ utmos_ liberality_

26-1. Of old, the rise of the Yin dynasty was due to I Chih who had served under the Hsia.

27-1. Hence it is only the enlightened ruler and the wise general who will use the highest intelligence of the army for purposes of spying and thereby they achieve great results.
