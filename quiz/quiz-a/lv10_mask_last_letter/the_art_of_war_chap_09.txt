IX.  THE ARMY ON THE MARCH
IX. TH_ ARM_ O_ TH_ MARC_

1-1. Sun Tzu said: We come now to the question of encamping the army, and observing signs of the enemy.
1-1. Su_ Tz_ said_ W_ com_ no_ t_ th_ questio_ o_ encampin_ th_ army_ an_ observin_ sign_ o_ th_ enemy_

1-2. Pass quickly over mountains, and keep in the neighborhood of valleys.
1-2. Pas_ quickl_ ove_ mountains_ an_ kee_ i_ th_ neighborhoo_ o_ valleys_

2-1. Camp in high places, facing the sun.
2-1. Cam_ i_ hig_ places_ facin_ th_ sun_

2-2. Do not climb heights in order to fight.
2-2. D_ no_ clim_ height_ i_ orde_ t_ fight_

2-3. So much for mountain warfare.
2-3. S_ muc_ fo_ mountai_ warfare_

3. After crossing a river, you should get far away from it.
3. Afte_ crossin_ _ river_ yo_ shoul_ ge_ fa_ awa_ fro_ it_

4-1. When an invading force crosses a river in its onward march, do not advance to meet it in mid-stream.
4-1. Whe_ a_ invadin_ forc_ crosse_ _ rive_ i_ it_ onwar_ march_ d_ no_ advanc_ t_ mee_ i_ i_ mid-stream_

4-2. It will be best to let half the army get across, and then deliver your attack.
4-2. I_ wil_ b_ bes_ t_ le_ hal_ th_ arm_ ge_ across_ an_ the_ delive_ you_ attack_

5. If you are anxious to fight, you should not go to meet the invader near a river which he has to cross.
5. I_ yo_ ar_ anxiou_ t_ fight_ yo_ shoul_ no_ g_ t_ mee_ th_ invade_ nea_ _ rive_ whic_ h_ ha_ t_ cross_

6-1. Moor your craft higher up than the enemy, and facing the sun.
6-1. Moo_ you_ craf_ highe_ u_ tha_ th_ enemy_ an_ facin_ th_ sun_

6-2. Do not move up-stream to meet the enemy.
6-2. D_ no_ mov_ up-strea_ t_ mee_ th_ enemy_

6-3. So much for river warfare.
6-3. S_ muc_ fo_ rive_ warfare_

7. In crossing salt-marshes, your sole concern should be to get over them quickly, without any delay.
7. I_ crossin_ salt-marshes_ you_ sol_ concer_ shoul_ b_ t_ ge_ ove_ the_ quickly_ withou_ an_ delay_

8-1. If forced to fight in a salt-marsh, you should have water and grass near you, and get your back to a clump of trees.
8-1. I_ force_ t_ figh_ i_ _ salt-marsh_ yo_ shoul_ hav_ wate_ an_ gras_ nea_ you_ an_ ge_ you_ bac_ t_ _ clum_ o_ trees_

8-2. So much for operations in salt-marches.
8-2. S_ muc_ fo_ operation_ i_ salt-marches_

9-1. In dry, level country, take up an easily accessible position with rising ground to your right and on your rear, so that the danger may be in front, and safety lie behind.
9-1. I_ dry_ leve_ country_ tak_ u_ a_ easil_ accessibl_ positio_ wit_ risin_ groun_ t_ you_ righ_ an_ o_ you_ rear_ s_ tha_ th_ dange_ ma_ b_ i_ front_ an_ safet_ li_ behind_

9-2. So much for campaigning in flat country.
9-2. S_ muc_ fo_ campaignin_ i_ fla_ country_

10. These are the four useful branches of military knowledge which enabled the Yellow Emperor to vanquish four several sovereigns.
10. Thes_ ar_ th_ fou_ usefu_ branche_ o_ militar_ knowledg_ whic_ enable_ th_ Yello_ Empero_ t_ vanquis_ fou_ severa_ sovereigns_

11. All armies prefer high ground to low and sunny places to dark.
11. Al_ armie_ prefe_ hig_ groun_ t_ lo_ an_ sunn_ place_ t_ dark_

12. If you are careful of your men, and camp on hard ground, the army will be free from disease of every kind, and this will spell victory.
12. I_ yo_ ar_ carefu_ o_ you_ men_ an_ cam_ o_ har_ ground_ th_ arm_ wil_ b_ fre_ fro_ diseas_ o_ ever_ kind_ an_ thi_ wil_ spel_ victory_

13-1. When you come to a hill or a bank, occupy the sunny side, with the slope on your right rear.
13-1. Whe_ yo_ com_ t_ _ hil_ o_ _ bank_ occup_ th_ sunn_ side_ wit_ th_ slop_ o_ you_ righ_ rear_

13-2. Thus you will at once act for the benefit of your soldiers and utilize the natural advantages of the ground.
13-2. Thu_ yo_ wil_ a_ onc_ ac_ fo_ th_ benefi_ o_ you_ soldier_ an_ utiliz_ th_ natura_ advantage_ o_ th_ ground_

14. When, in consequence of heavy rains up-country, a river which you wish to ford is swollen and flecked with foam, you must wait until it subsides.
14. When_ i_ consequenc_ o_ heav_ rain_ up-country_ _ rive_ whic_ yo_ wis_ t_ for_ i_ swolle_ an_ flecke_ wit_ foam_ yo_ mus_ wai_ unti_ i_ subsides_

15. Country in which there are precipitous cliffs with torrents running between, deep natural hollows, confined places, tangled thickets, quagmires and crevasses, should be left with all possible speed and not approached.
15. Countr_ i_ whic_ ther_ ar_ precipitou_ cliff_ wit_ torrent_ runnin_ between_ dee_ natura_ hollows_ confine_ places_ tangle_ thickets_ quagmire_ an_ crevasses_ shoul_ b_ lef_ wit_ al_ possibl_ spee_ an_ no_ approached_

16. While we keep away from such places, we should get the enemy to approach them; while we face them, we should let the enemy have them on his rear.
16. Whil_ w_ kee_ awa_ fro_ suc_ places_ w_ shoul_ ge_ th_ enem_ t_ approac_ them_ whil_ w_ fac_ them_ w_ shoul_ le_ th_ enem_ hav_ the_ o_ hi_ rear_

17. If in the neighborhood of your camp there should be any hilly country, ponds surrounded by aquatic grass, hollow basins filled with reeds, or woods with thick undergrowth, they must be carefully routed out and searched; for these are places where men in ambush or insidious spies are likely to be lurking.
17. I_ i_ th_ neighborhoo_ o_ you_ cam_ ther_ shoul_ b_ an_ hill_ country_ pond_ surrounde_ b_ aquati_ grass_ hollo_ basin_ fille_ wit_ reeds_ o_ wood_ wit_ thic_ undergrowth_ the_ mus_ b_ carefull_ route_ ou_ an_ searched_ fo_ thes_ ar_ place_ wher_ me_ i_ ambus_ o_ insidiou_ spie_ ar_ likel_ t_ b_ lurking_

18. When the enemy is close at hand and remains quiet, he is relying on the natural strength of his position.
18. Whe_ th_ enem_ i_ clos_ a_ han_ an_ remain_ quiet_ h_ i_ relyin_ o_ th_ natura_ strengt_ o_ hi_ position_

19. When he keeps aloof and tries to provoke a battle, he is anxious for the other side to advance.
19. Whe_ h_ keep_ aloo_ an_ trie_ t_ provok_ _ battle_ h_ i_ anxiou_ fo_ th_ othe_ sid_ t_ advance_

20. If his place of encampment is easy of access, he is tendering a bait.
20. I_ hi_ plac_ o_ encampmen_ i_ eas_ o_ access_ h_ i_ tenderin_ _ bait_

21-1. Movement amongst the trees of a forest shows that the enemy is advancing.
21-1. Movemen_ amongs_ th_ tree_ o_ _ fores_ show_ tha_ th_ enem_ i_ advancing_

21-2. The appearance of a number of screens in the midst of thick grass means that the enemy wants to make us suspicious.
21-2. Th_ appearanc_ o_ _ numbe_ o_ screen_ i_ th_ mids_ o_ thic_ gras_ mean_ tha_ th_ enem_ want_ t_ mak_ u_ suspicious_

22-1. The rising of birds in their flight is the sign of an ambuscade.
22-1. Th_ risin_ o_ bird_ i_ thei_ fligh_ i_ th_ sig_ o_ a_ ambuscade_

22-2. Startled beasts indicate that a sudden attack is coming.
22-2. Startle_ beast_ indicat_ tha_ _ sudde_ attac_ i_ coming_

23-1. When there is dust rising in a high column, it is the sign of chariots advancing; when the dust is low, but spread over a wide area, it betokens the approach of infantry.
23-1. Whe_ ther_ i_ dus_ risin_ i_ _ hig_ column_ i_ i_ th_ sig_ o_ chariot_ advancing_ whe_ th_ dus_ i_ low_ bu_ sprea_ ove_ _ wid_ area_ i_ betoken_ th_ approac_ o_ infantry_

23-2. When it branches out in different directions, it shows that parties have been sent to collect firewood.
23-2. Whe_ i_ branche_ ou_ i_ differen_ directions_ i_ show_ tha_ partie_ hav_ bee_ sen_ t_ collec_ firewood_

23-3. A few clouds of dust moving to and fro signify that the army is encamping.
23-3. _ fe_ cloud_ o_ dus_ movin_ t_ an_ fr_ signif_ tha_ th_ arm_ i_ encamping_

24-1. Humble words and increased preparations are signs that the enemy is about to advance.
24-1. Humbl_ word_ an_ increase_ preparation_ ar_ sign_ tha_ th_ enem_ i_ abou_ t_ advance_

24-2. Violent language and driving forward as if to the attack are signs that he will retreat.
24-2. Violen_ languag_ an_ drivin_ forwar_ a_ i_ t_ th_ attac_ ar_ sign_ tha_ h_ wil_ retreat_

25. When the light chariots come out first and take up a position on the wings, it is a sign that the enemy is forming for battle.
25. Whe_ th_ ligh_ chariot_ com_ ou_ firs_ an_ tak_ u_ _ positio_ o_ th_ wings_ i_ i_ _ sig_ tha_ th_ enem_ i_ formin_ fo_ battle_

26. Peace proposals unaccompanied by a sworn covenant indicate a plot.
26. Peac_ proposal_ unaccompanie_ b_ _ swor_ covenan_ indicat_ _ plot_

27. When there is much running about and the soldiers fall into rank, it means that the critical moment has come.
27. Whe_ ther_ i_ muc_ runnin_ abou_ an_ th_ soldier_ fal_ int_ rank_ i_ mean_ tha_ th_ critica_ momen_ ha_ come_

28. When some are seen advancing and some retreating, it is a lure.
28. Whe_ som_ ar_ see_ advancin_ an_ som_ retreating_ i_ i_ _ lure_

29. When the soldiers stand leaning on their spears, they are faint from want of food.
29. Whe_ th_ soldier_ stan_ leanin_ o_ thei_ spears_ the_ ar_ fain_ fro_ wan_ o_ food_

30. If those who are sent to draw water begin by drinking themselves, the army is suffering from thirst.
30. I_ thos_ wh_ ar_ sen_ t_ dra_ wate_ begi_ b_ drinkin_ themselves_ th_ arm_ i_ sufferin_ fro_ thirst_

31. If the enemy sees an advantage to be gained and makes no effort to secure it, the soldiers are exhausted.
31. I_ th_ enem_ see_ a_ advantag_ t_ b_ gaine_ an_ make_ n_ effor_ t_ secur_ it_ th_ soldier_ ar_ exhausted_

32-1. If birds gather on any spot, it is unoccupied.
32-1. I_ bird_ gathe_ o_ an_ spot_ i_ i_ unoccupied_

32-2. Clamor by night betokens nervousness.
32-2. Clamo_ b_ nigh_ betoken_ nervousness_

33-1. If there is disturbance in the camp, the general's authority is weak.
33-1. I_ ther_ i_ disturbanc_ i_ th_ camp_ th_ general'_ authorit_ i_ weak_

33-2. If the banners and flags are shifted about, sedition is afoot.
33-2. I_ th_ banner_ an_ flag_ ar_ shifte_ about_ seditio_ i_ afoot_

33-3. If the officers are angry, it means that the men are weary.
33-3. I_ th_ officer_ ar_ angry_ i_ mean_ tha_ th_ me_ ar_ weary_

34. When an army feeds its horses with grain and kills its cattle for food, and when the men do not hang their cooking-pots over the camp-fires, showing that they will not return to their tents, you may know that they are determined to fight to the death.
34. Whe_ a_ arm_ feed_ it_ horse_ wit_ grai_ an_ kill_ it_ cattl_ fo_ food_ an_ whe_ th_ me_ d_ no_ han_ thei_ cooking-pot_ ove_ th_ camp-fires_ showin_ tha_ the_ wil_ no_ retur_ t_ thei_ tents_ yo_ ma_ kno_ tha_ the_ ar_ determine_ t_ figh_ t_ th_ death_

35. The sight of men whispering together in small knots or speaking in subdued tones points to disaffection amongst the rank and file.
35. Th_ sigh_ o_ me_ whisperin_ togethe_ i_ smal_ knot_ o_ speakin_ i_ subdue_ tone_ point_ t_ disaffectio_ amongs_ th_ ran_ an_ file_

36. Too frequent rewards signify that the enemy is at the end of his resources; too many punishments betray a condition of dire distress.
36. To_ frequen_ reward_ signif_ tha_ th_ enem_ i_ a_ th_ en_ o_ hi_ resources_ to_ man_ punishment_ betra_ _ conditio_ o_ dir_ distress_

37. To begin by bluster, but afterwards to take fright at the enemy's numbers, shows a supreme lack of intelligence.
37. T_ begi_ b_ bluster_ bu_ afterward_ t_ tak_ frigh_ a_ th_ enemy'_ numbers_ show_ _ suprem_ lac_ o_ intelligence_

38. When envoys are sent with compliments in their mouths, it is a sign that the enemy wishes for a truce.
38. Whe_ envoy_ ar_ sen_ wit_ compliment_ i_ thei_ mouths_ i_ i_ _ sig_ tha_ th_ enem_ wishe_ fo_ _ truce_

39. If the enemy's troops march up angrily and remain facing ours for a long time without either joining battle or taking themselves off again, the situation is one that demands great vigilance and circumspection.
39. I_ th_ enemy'_ troop_ marc_ u_ angril_ an_ remai_ facin_ our_ fo_ _ lon_ tim_ withou_ eithe_ joinin_ battl_ o_ takin_ themselve_ of_ again_ th_ situatio_ i_ on_ tha_ demand_ grea_ vigilanc_ an_ circumspection_

40-1. If our troops are no more in number than the enemy, that is amply sufficient; it only means that no direct attack can be made.
40-1. I_ ou_ troop_ ar_ n_ mor_ i_ numbe_ tha_ th_ enemy_ tha_ i_ ampl_ sufficient_ i_ onl_ mean_ tha_ n_ direc_ attac_ ca_ b_ made_

40-2. What we can do is simply to concentrate all our available strength, keep a close watch on the enemy, and obtain reinforcements.
40-2. Wha_ w_ ca_ d_ i_ simpl_ t_ concentrat_ al_ ou_ availabl_ strength_ kee_ _ clos_ watc_ o_ th_ enemy_ an_ obtai_ reinforcements_

41. He who exercises no forethought but makes light of his opponents is sure to be captured by them.
41. H_ wh_ exercise_ n_ forethough_ bu_ make_ ligh_ o_ hi_ opponent_ i_ sur_ t_ b_ capture_ b_ them_

42-1. If soldiers are punished before they have grown attached to you, they will not prove submissive; and, unless submissive, then will be practically useless.
42-1. I_ soldier_ ar_ punishe_ befor_ the_ hav_ grow_ attache_ t_ you_ the_ wil_ no_ prov_ submissive_ and_ unles_ submissive_ the_ wil_ b_ practicall_ useless_

42-2. If, when the soldiers have become attached to you, punishments are not enforced, they will still be useless.
42-2. If_ whe_ th_ soldier_ hav_ becom_ attache_ t_ you_ punishment_ ar_ no_ enforced_ the_ wil_ stil_ b_ useless_

43-1. Therefore soldiers must be treated in the first instance with humanity, but kept under control by means of iron discipline.
43-1. Therefor_ soldier_ mus_ b_ treate_ i_ th_ firs_ instanc_ wit_ humanity_ bu_ kep_ unde_ contro_ b_ mean_ o_ iro_ discipline_

43-2. This is a certain road to victory.
43-2. Thi_ i_ _ certai_ roa_ t_ victory_

44. If in training soldiers commands are habitually enforced, the army will be well-disciplined; if not, its discipline will be bad.
44. I_ i_ trainin_ soldier_ command_ ar_ habituall_ enforced_ th_ arm_ wil_ b_ well-disciplined_ i_ not_ it_ disciplin_ wil_ b_ bad_

45. If a general shows confidence in his men but always insists on his orders being obeyed, the gain will be mutual.
45. I_ _ genera_ show_ confidenc_ i_ hi_ me_ bu_ alway_ insist_ o_ hi_ order_ bein_ obeyed_ th_ gai_ wil_ b_ mutual_
