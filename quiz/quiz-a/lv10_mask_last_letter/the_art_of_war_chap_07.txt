VII. MANEUVERING
VII. MANEUVERIN_

1. Sun Tzu said: In war, the general receives his commands from the sovereign.
1. Su_ Tz_ said_ I_ war_ th_ genera_ receive_ hi_ command_ fro_ th_ sovereign_

2. Having collected an army and concentrated his forces, he must blend and harmonize the different elements thereof before pitching his camp.
2. Havin_ collecte_ a_ arm_ an_ concentrate_ hi_ forces_ h_ mus_ blen_ an_ harmoniz_ th_ differen_ element_ thereo_ befor_ pitchin_ hi_ camp_

3-1. After that, comes tactical maneuvering, than which there is nothing more difficult.
3-1. Afte_ that_ come_ tactica_ maneuvering_ tha_ whic_ ther_ i_ nothin_ mor_ difficult_

3-2. The difficulty of tactical maneuvering consists in turning the devious into the direct, and misfortune into gain.
3-2. Th_ difficult_ o_ tactica_ maneuverin_ consist_ i_ turnin_ th_ deviou_ int_ th_ direct_ an_ misfortun_ int_ gain_

4. Thus, to take a long and circuitous route, after enticing the enemy out of the way, and though starting after him, to contrive to reach the goal before him, shows knowledge of the artifice of DEVIATION.
4. Thus_ t_ tak_ _ lon_ an_ circuitou_ route_ afte_ enticin_ th_ enem_ ou_ o_ th_ way_ an_ thoug_ startin_ afte_ him_ t_ contriv_ t_ reac_ th_ goa_ befor_ him_ show_ knowledg_ o_ th_ artific_ o_ DEVIATION_

5. Maneuvering with an army is advantageous; with an undisciplined multitude, most dangerous.
5. Maneuverin_ wit_ a_ arm_ i_ advantageous_ wit_ a_ undiscipline_ multitude_ mos_ dangerous_

6-1. If you set a fully equipped army in march in order to snatch an advantage, the chances are that you will be too late.
6-1. I_ yo_ se_ _ full_ equippe_ arm_ i_ marc_ i_ orde_ t_ snatc_ a_ advantage_ th_ chance_ ar_ tha_ yo_ wil_ b_ to_ late_

6-2. On the other hand, to detach a flying column for the purpose involves the sacrifice of its baggage and stores.
6-2. O_ th_ othe_ hand_ t_ detac_ _ flyin_ colum_ fo_ th_ purpos_ involve_ th_ sacrific_ o_ it_ baggag_ an_ stores_

7. Thus, if you order your men to roll up their buff-coats, and make forced marches without halting day or night, covering double the usual distance at a stretch, doing a hundred LI in order to wrest an advantage, the leaders of all your three divisions will fall into the hands of the enemy.
7. Thus_ i_ yo_ orde_ you_ me_ t_ rol_ u_ thei_ buff-coats_ an_ mak_ force_ marche_ withou_ haltin_ da_ o_ night_ coverin_ doubl_ th_ usua_ distanc_ a_ _ stretch_ doin_ _ hundre_ L_ i_ orde_ t_ wres_ a_ advantage_ th_ leader_ o_ al_ you_ thre_ division_ wil_ fal_ int_ th_ hand_ o_ th_ enemy_

8. The stronger men will be in front, the jaded ones will fall behind, and on this plan only one-tenth of your army will reach its destination.
8. Th_ stronge_ me_ wil_ b_ i_ front_ th_ jade_ one_ wil_ fal_ behind_ an_ o_ thi_ pla_ onl_ one-tent_ o_ you_ arm_ wil_ reac_ it_ destination_

9. If you march fifty LI in order to outmaneuver the enemy, you will lose the leader of your first division, and only half your force will reach the goal.
9. I_ yo_ marc_ fift_ L_ i_ orde_ t_ outmaneuve_ th_ enemy_ yo_ wil_ los_ th_ leade_ o_ you_ firs_ division_ an_ onl_ hal_ you_ forc_ wil_ reac_ th_ goal_

10. If you march thirty LI with the same object, two-thirds of your army will arrive.
10. I_ yo_ marc_ thirt_ L_ wit_ th_ sam_ object_ two-third_ o_ you_ arm_ wil_ arrive_

11. We may take it then that an army without its baggage-train is lost; without provisions it is lost; without bases of supply it is lost.
11. W_ ma_ tak_ i_ the_ tha_ a_ arm_ withou_ it_ baggage-trai_ i_ lost_ withou_ provision_ i_ i_ lost_ withou_ base_ o_ suppl_ i_ i_ lost_

12. We cannot enter into alliances until we are acquainted with the designs of our neighbors.
12. W_ canno_ ente_ int_ alliance_ unti_ w_ ar_ acquainte_ wit_ th_ design_ o_ ou_ neighbors_

13. We are not fit to lead an army on the march unless we are familiar with the face of the country-- its mountains and forests, its pitfalls and precipices, its marshes and swamps.
13. W_ ar_ no_ fi_ t_ lea_ a_ arm_ o_ th_ marc_ unles_ w_ ar_ familia_ wit_ th_ fac_ o_ th_ country-_ it_ mountain_ an_ forests_ it_ pitfall_ an_ precipices_ it_ marshe_ an_ swamps_

14. We shall be unable to turn natural advantage to account unless we make use of local guides.
14. W_ shal_ b_ unabl_ t_ tur_ natura_ advantag_ t_ accoun_ unles_ w_ mak_ us_ o_ loca_ guides_

15. In war, practice dissimulation, and you will succeed.
15. I_ war_ practic_ dissimulation_ an_ yo_ wil_ succeed_

16. Whether to concentrate or to divide your troops, must be decided by circumstances.
16. Whethe_ t_ concentrat_ o_ t_ divid_ you_ troops_ mus_ b_ decide_ b_ circumstances_

17. Let your rapidity be that of the wind, your compactness that of the forest.
17. Le_ you_ rapidit_ b_ tha_ o_ th_ wind_ you_ compactnes_ tha_ o_ th_ forest_

18. In raiding and plundering be like fire, in immovability like a mountain.
18. I_ raidin_ an_ plunderin_ b_ lik_ fire_ i_ immovabilit_ lik_ _ mountain_

19. Let your plans be dark and impenetrable as night, and when you move, fall like a thunderbolt.
19. Le_ you_ plan_ b_ dar_ an_ impenetrabl_ a_ night_ an_ whe_ yo_ move_ fal_ lik_ _ thunderbolt_

20. When you plunder a countryside, let the spoil be divided amongst your men; when you capture new territory, cut it up into allotments for the benefit of the soldiery.
20. Whe_ yo_ plunde_ _ countryside_ le_ th_ spoi_ b_ divide_ amongs_ you_ men_ whe_ yo_ captur_ ne_ territory_ cu_ i_ u_ int_ allotment_ fo_ th_ benefi_ o_ th_ soldiery_

21. Ponder and deliberate before you make a move.
21. Ponde_ an_ deliberat_ befor_ yo_ mak_ _ move_

22-1. He will conquer who has learnt the artifice of deviation.
22-1. H_ wil_ conque_ wh_ ha_ learn_ th_ artific_ o_ deviation_

22-2. Such is the art of maneuvering.
22-2. Suc_ i_ th_ ar_ o_ maneuvering_

23-1. The Book of Army Management says: On the field of battle, the spoken word does not carry far enough: hence the institution of gongs and drums.
23-1. Th_ Boo_ o_ Arm_ Managemen_ says_ O_ th_ fiel_ o_ battle_ th_ spoke_ wor_ doe_ no_ carr_ fa_ enough_ henc_ th_ institutio_ o_ gong_ an_ drums_

23-2. Nor can ordinary objects be seen clearly enough: hence the institution of banners and flags.
23-2. No_ ca_ ordinar_ object_ b_ see_ clearl_ enough_ henc_ th_ institutio_ o_ banner_ an_ flags_

24. Gongs and drums, banners and flags, are means whereby the ears and eyes of the host may be focused on one particular point.
24. Gong_ an_ drums_ banner_ an_ flags_ ar_ mean_ whereb_ th_ ear_ an_ eye_ o_ th_ hos_ ma_ b_ focuse_ o_ on_ particula_ point_

25-1. The host thus forming a single united body, is it impossible either for the brave to advance alone, or for the cowardly to retreat alone.
25-1. Th_ hos_ thu_ formin_ _ singl_ unite_ body_ i_ i_ impossibl_ eithe_ fo_ th_ brav_ t_ advanc_ alone_ o_ fo_ th_ cowardl_ t_ retrea_ alone_

25-2. This is the art of handling large masses of men.
25-2. Thi_ i_ th_ ar_ o_ handlin_ larg_ masse_ o_ men_

26. In night-fighting, then, make much use of signal-fires and drums, and in fighting by day, of flags and banners, as a means of influencing the ears and eyes of your army.
26. I_ night-fighting_ then_ mak_ muc_ us_ o_ signal-fire_ an_ drums_ an_ i_ fightin_ b_ day_ o_ flag_ an_ banners_ a_ _ mean_ o_ influencin_ th_ ear_ an_ eye_ o_ you_ army_

27. A whole army may be robbed of its spirit; a commander-in-chief may be robbed of his presence of mind.
27. _ whol_ arm_ ma_ b_ robbe_ o_ it_ spirit_ _ commander-in-chie_ ma_ b_ robbe_ o_ hi_ presenc_ o_ mind_

28. Now a soldier's spirit is keenest in the morning; by noonday it has begun to flag; and in the evening, his mind is bent only on returning to camp.
28. No_ _ soldier'_ spiri_ i_ keenes_ i_ th_ morning_ b_ noonda_ i_ ha_ begu_ t_ flag_ an_ i_ th_ evening_ hi_ min_ i_ ben_ onl_ o_ returnin_ t_ camp_

29-1. A clever general, therefore, avoids an army when its spirit is keen, but attacks it when it is sluggish and inclined to return.
29-1. _ cleve_ general_ therefore_ avoid_ a_ arm_ whe_ it_ spiri_ i_ keen_ bu_ attack_ i_ whe_ i_ i_ sluggis_ an_ incline_ t_ return_

29-2. This is the art of studying moods.
29-2. Thi_ i_ th_ ar_ o_ studyin_ moods_

30. Disciplined and calm, to await the appearance of disorder and hubbub amongst the enemy:-- this is the art of retaining self-possession.
30. Discipline_ an_ calm_ t_ awai_ th_ appearanc_ o_ disorde_ an_ hubbu_ amongs_ th_ enemy:-_ thi_ i_ th_ ar_ o_ retainin_ self-possession_

31. To be near the goal while the enemy is still far from it, to wait at ease while the enemy is toiling and struggling, to be well-fed while the enemy is famished:-- this is the art of husbanding one's strength.
31. T_ b_ nea_ th_ goa_ whil_ th_ enem_ i_ stil_ fa_ fro_ it_ t_ wai_ a_ eas_ whil_ th_ enem_ i_ toilin_ an_ struggling_ t_ b_ well-fe_ whil_ th_ enem_ i_ famished:-_ thi_ i_ th_ ar_ o_ husbandin_ one'_ strength_

32. To refrain from intercepting an enemy whose banners are in perfect order, to refrain from attacking an army drawn up in calm and confident array:-- this is the art of studying circumstances.
32. T_ refrai_ fro_ interceptin_ a_ enem_ whos_ banner_ ar_ i_ perfec_ order_ t_ refrai_ fro_ attackin_ a_ arm_ draw_ u_ i_ cal_ an_ confiden_ array:-_ thi_ i_ th_ ar_ o_ studyin_ circumstances_

33. It is a military axiom not to advance uphill against the enemy, nor to oppose him when he comes downhill.
33. I_ i_ _ militar_ axio_ no_ t_ advanc_ uphil_ agains_ th_ enemy_ no_ t_ oppos_ hi_ whe_ h_ come_ downhill_

34. Do not pursue an enemy who simulates flight; do not attack soldiers whose temper is keen.
34. D_ no_ pursu_ a_ enem_ wh_ simulate_ flight_ d_ no_ attac_ soldier_ whos_ tempe_ i_ keen_

35-1. Do not swallow bait offered by the enemy.
35-1. D_ no_ swallo_ bai_ offere_ b_ th_ enemy_

35-2. Do not interfere with an army that is returning home.
35-2. D_ no_ interfer_ wit_ a_ arm_ tha_ i_ returnin_ home_

36-1. When you surround an army, leave an outlet free.
36-1. Whe_ yo_ surroun_ a_ army_ leav_ a_ outle_ free_

36-2. Do not press a desperate foe too hard.
36-2. D_ no_ pres_ _ desperat_ fo_ to_ hard_

37. Such is the art of warfare.
37. Suc_ i_ th_ ar_ o_ warfare_
